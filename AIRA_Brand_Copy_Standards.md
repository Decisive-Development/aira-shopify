## 1. Positioning

**Category position:** the premium breathing ritual — the gap no competitor owns. Intake owns athlete-performance, Hostage owns young-DTC, Breathe Right owns pharmacy. aira is the considered, designed, keep-forever object in a category of disposable plastic.

**Proof layer:** the magnetic mechanism. The strip does the structural work; adhesive only holds position. Every claim ladders back to this.

**One-liner (internal):** A premium magnetic breathing system, engineered for nights and training — built for the Gulf.

**Performance is a use case, not the identity.** TRAIN sits beside SLEEP; neither swallows the brand. The roadmap (mouth tape, sleep mask, red light, electrolytes) hangs off "breathing ritual," not "sports gear."

## 2. Voice

**Register: knowledgeable friend who built it (decided 2026-06-12).** Precise, confident, evidence-led, but spoken, not written. The voice is a sharp friend who found something that works and explains why in plain words. Not a lab coat, not a worried caregiver, not marketing-speak. The read-aloud test is law: if you wouldn't say the sentence to someone's face, rewrite it. The luxury lives in the design, photography, and restraint, not in soft language.

Principles:

1. **Specific beats warm.** "The hold at 6am is the hold at 11pm" — not "supports your body's natural balance."
2. **Mechanism before promise.** Say *why* it works, then what it does. Never the reverse.
3. **Numbers earn their place.** Every stat has a named source (Dallam et al., 2018) or it doesn't ship. No source, no stat — ever (see Claims Register).
4. **Short sentences carry the weight.** One idea per sentence. Max ~3 lines per paragraph on mobile.
5. **Hype with specifics, not adjectives (revised 2026-06-12, Tom).** Energy and bold selling are welcome; this is a sales page, not a meditation app. But the hype is carried by specific true things (the hold, the reuse, real user words, the 30-night guarantee), never by empty intensifiers or invented numbers. Still banned: miracle, literally, 10x, "clinically proven" (no trials yet), "doctor recommended." Discouraged: revolutionary, game-changing — every TEMU knockoff uses them, so they read cheap, not exciting.
6. **Sell without apologizing.** CTAs are direct ("Get my kit"). We are a commercial brand; confidence is premium, hedging is not.

**Retired (from old Caregiver guidance):** mandatory soft verbs (support/restore/enhance), "we're on this journey together" energy, sensory-word quotas. Sensory words (flow, ease, breath) are allowed, not required.

**Medical line we never cross:** aira opens airways and supports nasal breathing. We do not treat, cure, or diagnose anything. Snoring is addressed as a mechanical consequence of mouth breathing — never as a medical condition we fix. (Verbs "fix/treat" stay banned for legal reasons, not tone.)

## 3. Audience priority

1. **Khalid** (36, Dubai entrepreneur, performance-minded) — primary voice target. If copy works for Khalid, it ships.
2. **Mohammed** (40–60, snoring, partner pressure) — served through SLEEP narrative and partner-noticed proof.
3. **Sara** (27–40, wellness-conscious professional) — served through design, ritual framing, and restraint; never through softened copy.

**Lead problem stack (decided 2026-06-12):** copy attacks problems in this order — snoring / blocked nose (incl. deviated septum) → deeper sleep → performance & recovery → no more mouth breathing. Problem-first structure: name the pain, agitate briefly, then mechanism, then outcome.

**Awareness level: COLD.** Assume the reader doesn't yet frame their tiredness, dry mouth, or snoring as a "nasal breathing problem." Copy starts at symptoms they recognize, then teaches the mechanism in one or two sentences, then differentiates. Never assume they know what a nasal strip is or why ours beats disposables.

**Top objection (attack first and hardest): will it stay on / is it comfortable.** Answer with the magnetic mechanism, 3M adhesive, and removal feel before price or brand-trust objections.

## 5. Mechanics

- **English:** UK spelling, always (colour, optimise, customise). GCC reads UK English. Never Americanise. One standard, no mixing. (Decided 2026-06-12; supersedes the v1 US assumption. Existing live copy gets swept for US spellings.)
- **Sentence case everywhere (extended 2026-06-12, Tom):** headings, buttons, badges, labels, link text. ("Everything you need for night one.", "30-night guarantee", "Research backed"). Terminal period on full-sentence headings, matches live site pattern. No Title Case anywhere; ALL CAPS only for eyebrow labels.
- **Eyebrows:** ALL CAPS, 1–3 words (SLEEP · TRAIN · THE AIRA DIFFERENCE · FAQ).
- **Numbered claims pattern:** bold problem label, period, then solution sentence. ("**Dry mouth.** Wake without the parched mouth…")
- **No em-dashes, no hyphens-as-pivots, no arrows (→)** anywhere in customer-facing copy (Tom, 2026-06-12: reads as AI). Pivot with a period, comma, or colon. Hyphens survive only inside compound words (sweat-proof, latex-free).
- **Numbers:** numerals always (4 strips, 30 pairs, 22%).
- **Exclamation marks:** no.
- **Stat cards:** H1 number → label → one-sentence finding → SOURCE, YEAR (the Dallam card is the canonical template).

Claims rule (restated, non-negotiable, re-raised and held 2026-06-12): every claim is (a) mechanically true, (b) backed by a real cited source, or (c) framed as what real users report. Nothing gets invented. No source, no stat. This explicitly covers invented result-stats: "9/10 people who snored before aira stopped after" does NOT ship unless it comes from a real documented survey. The legal ladder to the same selling power: real beta quotes → "partners usually notice before you do" → the 30-night guarantee as the put-up-or-shut-up line → survey the first 100 real customers, then use the real number with "in a survey of our first customers."

## 7. Copy patterns (the formulas that work — reuse them)

- **Headline formula:** mechanism contrast or concrete outcome, ≤10 words. ("The nasal strip that holds, because it doesn't pull.")
- **Section formula:** eyebrow → claim heading (2–4 words ×2 lines) → mechanism paragraph → 3 numbered problem→solution claims → CTA pair (conversion + conviction).
- **Proof order:** demonstration (before/after) → mechanism → sourced stat → testimonial. Never lead with testimonials.
- **CTA cadence:** one conversion CTA per scroll-depth "chapter"; the close is always the full offer (contents + guarantee + price), not a bare button.

## 8. GCC-aware rules (English-first)

- Write for translatability: no idioms that die in Arabic ("zero 3am surrender" is fine; "hits different" is not).
- Climate is a feature context: sweat-proof/humidity references land harder here than anywhere — use them.
- Ramadan: seasonal campaign angle (suhoor-to-iftar energy, family packs) — campaign copy only, not evergreen site copy.
- Proof localization over time: GCC customer reviews and local athletes outrank imported testimonials.
- Arabic site version: separate future project. No half-measures (no single translated page).

## 9. Tom's 5 copywriting rules (locked 2026-06-12)

1. **Spoken, not written.** Every line passes the read-aloud test. Contractions allowed. No corporate connective tissue ("furthermore," "in addition").
2. **No dashes or arrows.** No em-dash, no hyphen-as-pivot, no →. Periods, commas, colons. (Compound words keep their hyphens.)
3. **SEO keywords woven in naturally.** One primary keyword per page, 2–3 secondary. Keyword map comes from the 2026-06-12 deep research; keywords never bend a sentence out of spoken English. If keyword and voice conflict, voice wins on the homepage, keyword wins on SEO-intent pages (FAQ, blog).
4. **Problem-first.** Name the reader's problem before the product. Solution language stays mechanism-based; the verbs "fix/treat" remain banned for legal reasons even though the structure is fix-the-problem.
5. **Short beats thorough.** Cut every word that doesn't earn its place. Cold audience means we educate, but in single sentences, not paragraphs. One idea per sentence stands.
6. **Say what they want to hear, never imply a limit (Tom, 2026-06-12).** Confident adjectives are welcome when defensible ("proven" works because nasal dilation science exists and the Research backed badge links to it). Never phrase a promise so it hints at a boundary: "holds until morning" reads as a cap, "holds all night"
