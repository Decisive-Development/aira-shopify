1. PURPOSE
--------------------------------------------------
Replace the "Health Effects of poor airflow" section with a new
aspiration-led "Performance" section.

The old section was fear/education-led. The new one is aspiration-led
and targets the athletic / exercise audience (endurance — runners and
cyclists). The problem-agitation job that Health Effects used to do is
now carried by the comparison / stats table elsewhere on the page, so
THIS section does not need to educate or scare. It sells the upside.

Primary persona: Khalid — performance-minded, 20–45, GCC urban
professional / athlete.

3. LAYOUT — FULLSCREEN IMAGE HERO (not a box grid)
--------------------------------------------------
Decision: full-bleed photographic hero, single image.

Rationale: performance sells on emotion and aspiration. A fullscreen
image of an athlete under effort outperforms a modular feature grid,
and it deliberately breaks the page's box-grid rhythm to signal a
distinct pillar.

- Full-width, large vertical presence (~80–90vh on desktop, capped).
- Single hero image bleeds the full section.
- Text content sits on a dark negative-space third — camera-left,
  with the image subject offset camera-right.
- Content order, lower-left: eyebrow > headline > subhead >
  CTA (optional) > stat + benefit lines.

NOTE ON VISUAL FAMILY: this section intentionally diverges from the
navy bg-main + light-blue radial halo standard used on other custom
pages. The divergence is deliberate and approved — the performance
pillar is photographic, dark and energetic by design. Flag whether the
sections directly above/below need a transition treatment so the page
doesn't feel like it jumps.


4. IMAGE ART DIRECTION
--------------------------------------------------
Single hero image. Brief for the shoot / sourcing:

- Subject: ONE endurance athlete — runner or road cyclist. NOT a gym /
  lifting setting. Endurance reads "aerobic efficiency"; lifting reads
  "max strain" and fights the headline.

- Expression: calm, composed, focused. Effortless under effort.
  NOT grimacing or straining.

- MOUTH CLOSED. Non-negotiable. This is a nasal-breathing product; an
  open-mouthed, gasping athlete contradicts the entire message. The
  closed mouth is the proof of concept.

- The AIRA strip must be clearly visible on the bridge of the nose.
  Use a three-quarter or profile crop — the strip reads best from the
  side. Do not use a wide front-on body shot that loses the product.

- Light: dawn / early morning, directional. Premium and cinematic.
  Dawn also subtly links back to the sleep story (sleep well > train
  well).

- Tone: cool-to-neutral with deep shadow. This contrasts the warm
  amber of the sleep sections and provides a dark area for text.

- Composition: subject offset camera-right; clean dark third
  camera-left for copy. Slight motion blur in the background to imply
  movement; subject tack-sharp.

- Casting: cast for the GCC market — the model should reflect the
  target customer.

- Optional: a small product detail inset if the strip does not read
  clearly at hero scale.

CONSTRAINT: this cannot be stock — no stock athlete is wearing an AIRA
strip. It requires a custom shoot OR a careful retouch of the product
onto a licensed image. This is a cost + timeline dependency (see §8).


5. COPY (final — design may tweak length, not claims)
--------------------------------------------------
Eyebrow      : TRAIN

Headline     : More air. Less effort.

Subhead      : AIRA holds your nasal passages open so you breathe
               deeper through your nose — the way your body is built to
               work under load. More efficient airflow, less wasted
               energy, every session.

CTA (optional): "Shop AIRA" — confirm whether this section needs its
               own CTA or relies on the page-level CTA.

PROOF STAT (the visual anchor of the section):
  Value         : 22%
  Label         : More efficient breathing
  Supporting line: Recreational runners moved the same air with up to
                   22% less effort after switching to nasal breathing —
                   with no drop in VO2max.
  Footnote      : Dallam et al., 2018
  Footnote URL  : https://journals.aiac.org.au/index.php/IJKSS/article/view/4400

  CRITICAL: the 22% must stay tethered to "recreational runners" + the
  endurance context. Do NOT crop it to a standalone "22% MORE EFFICIENT"
  hero number — out of context that is an over-claim. Keep the
  qualifying line attached to the number.

THREE BENEFIT LINES (icon-led, short):
  - Easier endurance — an open airway means less breathing effort on
    long, steady efforts.
  - Cleaner air in — your nose filters, warms and humidifies every
    breath. Your mouth doesn't.
  - Calmer recovery — nasal breathing helps bring heart rate and
    breathing back down between efforts.

  Note: "Calmer recovery" is a qualitative benefit, not a stat — keep
  it as words, no number behind it.

ONLY ONE STATISTIC in this section, by design. We have one strong
peer-reviewed study; a stat wall stretched from a single paper reads
thin and invites scrutiny. One real number beats three asterisked
ones (cf. competitor HiStrips' 96% / 91% / 94% self-reported survey
stats — we are deliberately not copying that approach).

7. VOICE CHECK
--------------------------------------------------
AIRA voice = warm, human, calm, science-simplified — never salesy,
clinical or guru-like. The copy above is intentionally punchy but
should not be pushed harder into hype. If any element reads as boastful
or bro-y in design, pull it back toward calm confidence.
